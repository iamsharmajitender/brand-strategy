# POV — Point of View

**Core question:** What belief am I challenging?

Use this voice when you want to take a position, challenge conventional wisdom, and reframe how your audience thinks about a problem.

## When to use POV

- You disagree with a common industry belief
- You want to establish thought leadership
- The topic needs a sharp, memorable take — not a neutral summary

## Structure

Follow this flow in order:

1. **Hook** — Open with a strong belief or challenge
2. **Sharp statement** — State your position in one clear line
3. **Why this is misunderstood** — 2–3 lines on the common mistake
4. **What actually matters** — Reframe the real problem
5. **Enterprise consequence** — Why this matters at scale
6. **Closing line** — End with a memorable principle

## Template

Copy this block and replace the bracketed placeholders:

```text
[Strong claim]

Most teams think [common belief].
But the real problem is [deeper issue].

At enterprise scale, this creates [risk or failure].

The real architecture shift is not [surface thing].
It is [deeper architectural truth].

[Memorable closing principle]
```

## Examples

### Example 1 — RAG

> RAG is not an architecture strategy.
>
> Most teams think adding a vector database makes enterprise AI production-ready. It doesn't.
>
> RAG solves retrieval. It does not solve policy, correctness, or decision authority.
>
> At enterprise scale, this creates hidden risk: similarity gets treated as truth.
>
> The real architecture shift is not retrieval augmentation. It is governance augmentation.
>
> **Retrieval informs. Deterministic systems decide.**

### Example 2 — Agents

> Agents without governance are enterprise risk multipliers.
>
> Most teams are focused on agent autonomy. But autonomy without policy is just uncontrolled execution.
>
> At enterprise scale, this breaks auditability, compliance, and trust.
>
> The shift is not from prompts to agents. It is from isolated intelligence to governed orchestration.
>
> **Agents should plan. Systems should govern.**

## Pre-publish checklist

- [ ] Opens with a clear, challengeable claim — not a vague observation
- [ ] Names the common belief you are pushing against
- [ ] Reframes the problem, not just criticizes the status quo
- [ ] Includes an enterprise-scale consequence
- [ ] Ends with one memorable line the reader can repeat
