# EXP — Explain

**Core question:** How do I simplify this without losing truth?

Use this voice when the topic is dense, jargon-heavy, or widely misunderstood. Your job is to make it accessible to a smart audience — not to oversimplify or hide nuance.

## When to use EXP

- The topic has too much jargon or conflicting definitions
- Smart readers are confused, not beginners
- You need to translate complexity into a clear mental model

## Structure

Follow this flow in order:

1. **What people get wrong** — The common simplification or misconception
2. **The simple truth** — One sentence that captures the real idea
3. **The mental model** — An analogy, diagram, or framing that sticks
4. **What you must not lose** — The nuance that separates accuracy from oversimplification
5. **What to do with this** — One practical takeaway

## Template

Copy this block and replace the bracketed placeholders:

```text
Most explanations of [topic] stop at [oversimplified version].

That's useful, but incomplete.

The simple truth:
[One clear sentence that captures the real idea]

Think of it like this:
[Mental model or analogy]

What you must not lose:
* [Nuance 1]
* [Nuance 2]

What to do with this:
[Practical takeaway]
```

## Examples

### Example 1 — RAG

> Most explanations of RAG stop at "add a vector database and retrieve relevant chunks."
>
> That's useful, but incomplete.
>
> **The simple truth:** RAG is a retrieval layer — it finds information. It does not decide what is true, allowed, or authoritative.
>
> **Think of it like this:** RAG is a research assistant, not a judge. It brings documents to the table. Something else must decide what counts.
>
> **What you must not lose:**
>
> - Retrieval quality ≠ answer correctness
> - Similarity ≠ truth
> - Access ≠ authority
>
> **What to do with this:** Treat RAG as an input stage. Put validation, policy, and identity upstream or downstream — not inside the retrieval step.

### Example 2 — Agents

> Most explanations of AI agents stop at "give the model tools and let it act."
>
> That's useful, but incomplete.
>
> **The simple truth:** An agent is a planner that proposes actions. Execution, permission, and audit still belong to the system.
>
> **Think of it like this:** An agent is a project manager, not a contractor. It coordinates work. It should not unilaterally sign contracts.
>
> **What you must not lose:**
>
> - Autonomy without boundaries is risk
> - Tool access is a permission problem, not a model problem
> - Every action needs a trace
>
> **What to do with this:** Design the agent as one layer in a governed stack — not as a standalone decision-maker.

## Pre-publish checklist

- [ ] Names the oversimplification you are correcting
- [ ] Delivers one clear "simple truth" sentence
- [ ] Includes a mental model the reader can remember
- [ ] Explicitly preserves nuance — what not to lose
- [ ] Ends with something actionable, not just conceptual
